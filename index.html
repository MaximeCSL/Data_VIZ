Visualizations

The bars are descending in order, starting with Technology sector which has the highest return and going down to Crude Oil, which has a negative return.

We see that Technologie sector has the highest annualized return at 24.2% and a high Sharpe Ratio of 96, meaning it has provided good returns relative to its risk. In contrast, Brazil Stock has experienced a negative return of -6.3% and a negative Sharpe Ratio, indicating poor performance when adjusting for risk. On the other hand, US Treasury Bond ETF shows a negative return, reflecting a challenging period for those bonds. But we have to choose real bond value, not available in Yfinance API.

The Value at Risk (VaR) is -13.48%, meaning there's a 5% chance of returns being worse than this. The Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR) is even lower at -18.22%, indicating the average loss in the worst 5% of scenarios. On the upside, the Max Return at 5% is 23.87%, with an average of the best returns at 28.62%.

Interpretations

In comparing different asset classes, the S&P 500, equities, often shows superior long-term returns but with higher volatility compared to specially constructed indices like price-weighted or equal-weighted ones. While these alternative indices offer their own advantages, such as more balanced representation or reduced size bias, they typically do not outperform the S&P 500, which benefits from its focus on larger, more established companies. Futhermore, Markowitz portfolio optimization can yield even higher returns than the S&P 500 by effectively balancing risk and return, it introduces additional complexities such as transaction costs and other operational expenses that are not factored into the theoretical model. These real-world considerations can potentially offset some of the theoretical advantages of a Markowitz-optimized portfolio.
